# Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887–1920)

Srinivasa Ramanujan was an Indian mathematician widely regarded as one of the most extraordinary mathematical geniuses in history. Born on December 22, 1887, in Erode, Tamil Nadu, he produced groundbreaking work with almost no formal training, developing thousands of results that mathematicians are still unpacking today.

## Early Life and Self-Discovery

Ramanujan grew up in modest circumstances in Kumbakonam. By age 12, he had mastered advanced trigonometry on his own. His defining early influence was *A Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics* by G.S. Carr — a dry compendium of theorems without proofs. Rather than being deterred, Ramanujan used it as a launchpad, independently proving and extending nearly everything in it.

His obsession with mathematics came at a cost: he failed out of college twice, unable to pass subjects outside mathematics.

## The Letter That Changed Everything

In 1913, working as a low-paid clerk at the Madras Port Trust, Ramanujan wrote a now-famous letter to G.H. Hardy, a leading mathematician at Cambridge. The letter contained roughly 120 theorems — many unlike anything Hardy had seen. Hardy initially suspected a fraud or a crank, but on closer examination wrote that the results "must be true, because, if they were not true, no one would have had the imagination to invent them."

Hardy arranged for Ramanujan to come to Cambridge, beginning one of the most celebrated collaborations in mathematical history.

## Contributions

Ramanujan's work spanned several areas, often producing results of stunning depth from pure intuition:

- **Number theory** — His work on partition functions (counting the ways an integer can be written as a sum of positive integers) led to the Hardy-Ramanujan asymptotic formula.
- **Infinite series** — He discovered extraordinarily fast-converging series for computing π, some of which underlie modern record-setting computations.
- **Mock theta functions** — Introduced near the end of his life in a letter to Hardy, these puzzled mathematicians for nearly 80 years until their deep structure was finally explained in the early 2000s via the theory of mock modular forms.
- **Taxicab numbers** — The famous anecdote: Hardy visited Ramanujan in the hospital and mentioned his taxi number, 1729, was "dull." Ramanujan immediately replied it was the smallest number expressible as the sum of two cubes in two different ways (1³ + 12³ = 9³ + 10³).
- **Ramanujan's conjecture** — His conjecture about the tau function, proven decades later, became a cornerstone of modern number theory and contributed to the Langlands program.

## The Nature of His Genius

What makes Ramanujan singular is the *manner* in which he worked. He claimed many results came to him in dreams, delivered by the Hindu goddess Namagiri. He often stated results without proof, and in several documented cases, his "intuitions" turned out to be provably correct but required decades of new mathematics to verify.

Hardy placed him in the same tier as Euler and Jacobi, giving him a perfect score of 100 on his personal 0–100 scale of mathematical talent — while modestly giving himself a 25.

## Final Years and Death

Ramanujan's time in England (1914–1919) was intellectually prolific but physically devastating. He struggled with the cold climate, dietary restrictions as a devout Brahmin, and the strains of wartime. He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1918 — one of the youngest ever — but his health collapsed. He returned to India in 1919 and died on April 26, 1920, at just 32 years old, likely from hepatic amoebiasis or tuberculosis.

## Legacy

Ramanujan left behind roughly **3,900 results** across three notebooks and loose papers. The "Lost Notebook," rediscovered in 1976, alone contained hundreds of unpublished findings that are still generating active research. Fields like string theory, cryptography, and computer science have all touched his work.

His life is the subject of the biography *The Man Who Knew Infinity* (1991) by Robert Kanigel, later adapted into a 2015 film. December 22 is celebrated as **National Mathematics Day** in India in his honor.

Ramanujan stands as a testament to the idea that mathematical truth is, in some sense, independent of formal education — accessible to a mind attuned enough to hear it.
